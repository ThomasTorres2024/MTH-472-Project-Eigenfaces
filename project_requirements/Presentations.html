Presentations

Presentations (25% of the total grade): The presentations are intended to be completed in groups of two (or if needed three) students with oral presentations to be given during the last 1.5 weeks of classes Tues. Dec. 2, Thurs.  Dec. 4, and Tues. Dec. 9 (there can be some limited flexibility on groups, though not knowing anyone in class or not liking to be in a group is not a valid reason).

Primary Objective: Give an oral presentation in class with prepared slides.

  1. You will work in a group - presentation will be a maximum of 20-30 minutes. After each presentation 5-10 minutes will reserved for questions by students and the instructor. 
  2. All students must present, each speaking roughly the equal amount of time.
  3. Your slides must be prepared using some sort of typesetting software: LaTeX/Beamer or Power Point/Word. If you need help with typesetting math equations, reach out to me sooner rather than later. Poorly designed slides will result in a lower final grade.
  4. Your presentation will include (a) clear statement of the problem;  (b) motivation/application; (c) theoretical background/numerical methods; (d) short analysis; and (e) at least one example.
  5. No written report is required.
  6. More details (rubric, etc.) and presentation ideas are given in the Brightspace Module - Presentations

Some Ideas for Presentations (due Thurs. Oct. 23, 5pm) - there are many, many more ideas beyond what is listed

  • Expand and cover in more depth any topic in the class. This is a survey class and mostly a surface covering of topics are presented.

  • Matrix completion is the problem of recovering a low-rank matrix from only a subset of its observed entries, with applications in areas such as recommender systems, computer vision, and signal processing. 

    Cai, J.F., Candès, E.J. and Shen, Z., 2010. A singular value thresholding algorithm for matrix completion. SIAM Journal on optimization, 20(4), pp.1956-1982.

  • Introduce Ill-posed problems - describe what they are and how to solve them - particularly linear systems with "noise"  in the right-hand side of the linear system. Describe regularization methods and the toolbox in Matlab.  A good starting point is the following "recent" thesis:

    1. Arthur Neuman, Regularization Methods for Ill-posed Problems 2010 MS Thesis, Kent State Univeristy

    2. P.C. Hansen,The L-curve and its use in the numerical treatment of inverse problems

  • Give an overview of the main linear algebra tools used by Google. A good starting point is the following paper:

    1. Kurt Bryan and Tanya Leise, The $25,000,000,000 Eigenvector: The Linear Algebra behind Google, SIAM Review, 48 (3): pp. 569–581, 2006.

  • Introduce the notion of a companion matrix of a scalar polynomial and prove certain properties about it. Connect this to the MATLAB routine roots. Discuss the existence of different companion matrices and its importance. You can start with Wikipedia and/or type help roots in MATLAB.

  • The Fisher information matrix quantifies the amount of information an observable random variable carries about unknown parameters of a statistical model, capturing the sensitivity of the likelihood function to parameter changes. It plays a central role in estimation theory. In AI, the Fisher information matrix is often used to measure parameter sensitivity in deep learning models, helping to identify which parameters are most critical for maintaining performance. Fisher Matrix for Beginners, by  D. Wittman   
  • Discuss symmetric eigenvalue problem and/or properties of the symmetric tridiagonal matrix. An entire textbook is dedicated to the symmetric eigenvalue problems: Beresford Parlett, The Symmetric Eigenvalue problem, SIAM 1998.

  • Discuss the connection between matrices and graphs/networks. Talk about eigenvector centrality and  the importance of the Perron-Frobenius theorem. Provide an example similar to Matlab's Karate demo

  • Give an overview of Krylov subspace methods. A good starting point is the following paper:

    1. Ilse C. F. Ipsen and Carl Meyer,  The Idea Behind Krylov Methods, The American Mathematical Monthly, 105:10 (1998): pp. 889-899.

  • Review the basic ideas behind singular value decomposition (SVD). Describe how SVD is applied to problems involving image processing – in particular, how SVD aids the calculation of so-called eigenfaces, which provide an efficient representation of facial im- ages in face recognition. A great starting point is the following article:

    Neil Muller, Lourenco Magaia, and B. M. Herbst, Singular Value Decomposition, Eigenfaces, and 3D Reconstructions, SIAM Review, 46 (3): pp. 518 – 545, 2004.

    M. Turk and A. Pentland, Eigenfaces for recognition, J. Cognitive Neurosci., 3 (1991)
  • Introduce and describe the generalized eigenvalue and/or generalized singular value decomposition problems. An interesting problem related to smoothing and removing noise from data by applying generalized singular value decomposition is presented in the undergraduate thesis: 

    GFS Boisramé, THE USE OF LINEAR ALGEBRA IN MODELING THE PROBABILITIES OF PREDICTED FUTURE OCCURRENCES, 2010

  • Describe how to define a matrix exponential and discuss its role in finding solutions to linear systems of differential equations. You can start by using Google, more or less any differential equations textbook.

  • Describe MATLAB command subspace and how this is used to compute angles between subspaces (also vectors - subspace span by a single vector). Notice that normal procedure for vectors dot product of two normalize vectors can be inaccurate for small angles.  A good starting point is the following article:

    1. A. V. Knyazev and M. E. Argentati, Principal Angles between Subspaces in an A-Based Scalar Product: Algorithms and Perturbation Estimates. SIAM Journal on Scientific Computing, 23 (2002).

  • Introduce the notion of a stochastic matrix and discuss some of its properties. Then apply these results in connection to Markov chains. A good starting point is an excerpt from “Linear Algebra and Its Applications” by J. Holt.

  • Describe how the Kronecker product of two matrices is defined and prove several properties about it. Discuss the application of Kronecker product to for solving matrix equation AXB = C, where A,B,C are known matrices and X is an unknown matrix.

  • Introduce the notion of Hessian matrix of a function in n variables. Discuss the properties of a Hessian matrix and introduce the notions of positive definite, negative, and indefinite matrices. Finally, discuss the Second Derivative Test for Extrema of Multi- variable Functions. 

  • Introduce what is a Householder transformation and discuss its geometric description and discuss how Matlab;s QR function works. Describe the few areas where Householder matrices are used and give a short explanation how and why they are used there. For example you could look into QR-method for computing the eigenvalues of a matrix.

  • Discuss the notion of matrix functions. A good starting point would be selected sections of the article below.

    Nick Higham and Lijing Lin, Matrix Functions: A Short Course, MIMS EPrint 2013.73, Manchester Institute for Mathematical Sciences, The University of Manchester, UK, November 2013.

    Describe what is Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and discuss some applications. Describe the connection between PCA and SVD. A great starting point is “A Tutorial on Principal Component Analysis”

  • Discuss various generalizations of least squares and their applications and Matlab related commands  lsqr and pinv. What are some common algorithms for solving these problems? How does one solve rank-deficient least squares problems?

  • Discuss numerical algorithms for finding solutions to linear systems or for finding eigenvalues. Good starting point is any textbook on numerical linear algebra or matrix algorithms.

  • Pick a Matlab command associated with numerical linear algebra (not plot) and thoroughly discuss the algorithm(s) behind them, e.g. eig, eigs, svd, svds, schur, qr, qz, rsf2csf, hess, gsvd,lu, ldl, chol, qrinsert, normest.

  • Discuss the history and development of BLAS (Basic Linear Algebra Subroutines) or LAPACK (Linear Algebra Package).

  • Discuss randomized numerical methods:  Randomized algorithms for matrices and data

Abstract for the Presentations (due Thurs. Nov. 13, 5pm)

An abstract is a short document that is intended to capture the interest of a potential attendee to your presentation. In a sense it is the marketing document for your presentation that must engage the reader by telling them what your presentation is about and why they should attend.

 

Your submission should contain the title of your presentation and the abstract limited to 100-200 words. An abstract with a few words more or less is fine, but not a lot!

 

Please format your abstract submission as follows:

  • Title of your talk at the very top, centered (one or more lines)
  • Names of authors/presenters on the line below the title (also centered)
  • Skip one or two lines and start your abstract.
  • Submit your abstract as a single pdf.
  • Note that all the abstracts will be compiled into a single pdf file and shared with all students before the final presentations. 

 

Here are some things to keep in mind. Note that NOT all of the components listed below need to be included in your abstract or are applicable to your presentation. This is just a list of few things to keep in mind and is not exhaustive.

 

From idea to writing…

 

  • When sitting down to write the abstract, remember to keep your audience in mind and to be clear and concise (select only what you need to make a point) and engaging!

 

  • Consider the following content…
    • Description: a description of your presentation.
    • Lessons learned: conclusions/outcomes/learnings and implications of the final project that you perceive important for the audience.
    • Practical Applications: how might the audience be able use results you outlined.
    • Next Steps: possible next steps for implementation and further study.

 

  • Choose a title that will sell!
    • Sometimes the title comes after the abstract’s been written… a nice way to tie it all together… but do what works for you!
    • Your title should be simple, descriptive and engaging – it is your mini-advertisement! Again, not crucial, but give it a try!

Draft of Presentation Slides (due Thurs. Nov. 13, 5pm)

For purposes of this submission, your presentation slides should start to take some shape by now. It is in your interest to provide me with as complete of a draft as you can. That way I can provide you more feedback ensuring that your final presentation goes as smoothly as possible. Note that the main objective of this part of the project is to make it easier for you at the end.

 

This portion of the project is essentially, all or no credit.

 

You draft should contain at least the following pages: 

 

  • Front page – include the title of your talk, date, and name(s) of presenter(s).
  • Introduction/Motivation – one or more slides. At least have one slide and include a bulleted list on what you would like to talk about.
  • Problem Statement – it is important to have this clear at this stage and be able to describe it. Try to keep this part to one or two slides.
  • Details on your solution/approaches – Here try to do as much as you can. At the very least it would be good to include some handwritten notes, if not slides. Submit those as pdf and preferably shuffled in the correct order.
  • Examples: think of one or more examples that you think that you may want to include.  This can be one or more slides. If nothing else, at least have a blank slide and write something like “Here goes an example”.
  • Software Demo: Whether you will have this part or not depends on your project, your strengths and interests, etc. It is not required. But if you are planning on demonstrating something, then just include a slide with something like “Here goes software demo”.
  • Conclusion: Have at least a blank slide, preferably with some wording.
  • Bibliography: Include a reference list used to prepare your presentation. 

 

 

IMPORTANT NOTE: While you are preparing your slides, I imagine that you will be using either LaTeX/Matlab or Power Point.

 

  • LaTeX: There is a really nice class called Beamer that allows you to make slides very easily using LaTeX syntax. If you need help getting started, then just let me know. If you have never used LaTeX, then I do NOT recommend this approach.
  • Microsoft Power Point: This is a standard presentation tool freely available to URI students (part of Office 365). Typesetting math can be a bit cumbersome, but doable. Also, if you handwrite exactly and send me the picture of what you want, I am happy to help you create it using LaTeX, which will produce pdf, and then you can use screenshot and copy/paste the image into your presentation. It is not ideal, but it will suffice.
